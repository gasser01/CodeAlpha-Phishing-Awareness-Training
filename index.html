01 · The basics

What is phishing?

Phishing is a social-engineering attack where a criminal pretends to be someone you trust — your bank, your boss, a delivery company, a colleague — to trick you into handing over passwords, card numbers or money, or into installing malware. The message is the bait; you are the target.

🎣

1. The lure

An email, text or call posing as a trusted source, often with a sense of urgency.

→
🔗

2. The hook

A malicious link, fake login page or infected attachment you're pushed to click.

→
🔑

3. The catch

You enter credentials or run the file — the attacker now has access.

→
💰

4. The payday

Stolen data, drained accounts, ransomware or a foothold into your company.

Why it works: phishing doesn't attack your computer — it attacks your trust, fear and hurry. No firewall blocks a human decision, which is why awareness is your strongest defence.
02 · Know your enemy

The many faces of phishing

Phishing isn't just email. Attackers adapt the same trick to every channel you use.

📧

Email phishing

Mass-sent fake emails impersonating brands or services, hoping a fraction of recipients click.

🎯

Spear phishing

A targeted attack crafted for one person using details from social media or leaks.

👑

Whaling

Spear phishing aimed at "big fish" — CEOs, CFOs and executives — for high-value fraud.

📱

Smishing

Phishing by SMS: "Your parcel is held, pay the fee here" with a malicious short link.

📞

Vishing

Voice phishing — a phone call from "your bank's fraud team" pressuring you to act.

💼

BEC

Business Email Compromise: a spoofed boss emails "urgently wire this invoice." Costs billions yearly.

🔍

Clone phishing

A real email you received, copied and re-sent with the link swapped for a malicious one.

❄

Angler phishing

Fake "support" accounts on social media replying to your complaints to steal your login.

04 · Look-alikes

Spotting fake websites

Phishing links lead to convincing fake login pages. The URL almost always gives it away — read it right to left.

The URLVerdictWhy
https://paypa1.com/login Fake A digit 1 replacing the letter l — classic typosquatting.
https://paypal.com.secure-login.ru Fake The real domain is secure-login.ru. "paypal.com" is only a subdomain — read the part just before the first single slash.
http://paypal-account-verify.com Fake Extra words bolted onto the brand, and plain http (no padlock) for a login page.
https://xn--pypal-4ve.com Fake A "punycode" homograph — non-Latin letters that look like "paypal" in the address bar.
https://www.paypal.com/signin Real The registered domain immediately left of the first slash is exactly paypal.com.

Read every link right-to-left:

  1. Find the first single / after https://.
  2. The two words just before it are the real domain (e.g. paypal.com).
  3. Everything else — subdomains, paths, hyphens — can say anything. Ignore it.
The padlock myth: a 🔒 HTTPS padlock only means the connection is encrypted — not that the site is honest. Most phishing sites now use HTTPS too. The padlock is not a trust badge.
05 · The psychology

Social-engineering tactics

Attackers pull the same psychological levers every time. Name the trick and it loses its power.

⏱

Urgency

"Act in 24 hours or lose access." A ticking clock stops you from checking.

🔥

Fear

"Suspicious login detected!" Panic makes you click first and think later.

👑

Authority

Posing as your CEO, IT department or police so you don't question the request.

🎁

Greed / reward

"You've won a prize / a refund is waiting." A tempting payoff lowers your guard.

🤝

Trust & familiarity

Spoofing a brand, colleague or friend you already know and rarely double-check.

🧹

Curiosity

"Here's the photo of you..." or a mystery invoice you feel compelled to open.

06 · It really happens

Real-world phishing attacks

These weren't careless people — they were targeted, convincing attacks on major organisations.

2013

Target — 40M cards stolen

Attackers phished a HVAC contractor for network credentials, then pivoted into Target's payment systems. 40 million card numbers exposed; ~$292M in costs.

2016

The "Google Docs" worm

A fake Google Docs share asked users to grant app permissions. ~1 million Gmail accounts hit in one hour before Google killed it.

2016

Podesta / DNC email leak

A fake "reset your Google password" email led to a campaign chairman's inbox being copied — a spear-phish with global political fallout.

2013–15

Google & Facebook — $100M BEC

One man sent fake invoices impersonating a real hardware supplier. Two tech giants wired him over $100 million before it was caught.

2020

Twitter admin-tool hack

Vishing (phone phishing) of employees gave attackers internal tools — verified accounts of Obama, Musk & Apple tweeted a Bitcoin scam.

2022

Uber breach

An attacker spammed an employee with MFA push prompts ("MFA fatigue"), then posed as IT on WhatsApp until they approved one. Full internal access followed.

07 · Your defence

Best practices & quick tips

Build these habits and the overwhelming majority of phishing simply bounces off you.

✅ Do

  • Stop & slow down. Urgency is the attacker's weapon — remove it.
  • Hover before you click to preview the real link destination.
  • Go direct. Type the company's address yourself instead of clicking.
  • Verify out-of-band. Phone the sender on a number you already trust.
  • Turn on MFA everywhere — ideally an app or hardware key, not SMS.
  • Use a password manager. It won't autofill on a fake domain — a built-in alarm.
  • Report it to your IT/security team, then delete.
  • Keep software & browsers updated so malicious attachments fail.

❌ Don't

  • Don't trust the display name — check the actual email address.
  • Don't open unexpected attachments, especially .zip, .html or macro files.
  • Don't enter credentials from a link in an email — ever.
  • Don't approve an MFA prompt you didn't trigger. Deny and report it.
  • Don't reuse passwords — one phish shouldn't unlock everything.
  • Don't reply with personal or payment details, even if it "looks official."
  • Don't let the padlock fool you — HTTPS ≠ honest.
  • Don't be embarrassed to ask "is this real?" Asking beats clicking.
🧠

The one rule to remember: if a message creates urgency and asks you to click, log in, pay or share something — pause and verify through a channel you trust.
